India to Bharat · Essay #202

How do our present ‘rulers’ – promoted, selected or elected – compare with erstwhile ‘Rajas’?

1. Our forefathers were governed by traditional ‘Raja’, rulers, who had to have their code of ethics and conduct, their swadharma. Today the power and responsibility to govern us rest on the shoulders of the elected representatives, bureaucracy and professionals. We know quite a lot about how our self-centred modern ‘rajas’ act and behave, focused as they’re on immediate gratification and short-term vision! We’re not aware of any code of ethics applicable to our rulers, but what is expected from a Raja, in the Bharatiya tradition has been clearly laid down.

2. Rajdharma: The ideal of a ruler is best expressed by Bheeshma as he advises Yudhishthira, in the Mahabharat. He says: The ‘ruler’ has to know and follow the laws of Dharma; when he does so, then the people will also follow their respective dharma. An important responsibility of a ruler is to spread Spirituality, Truth, Justice in society. However, if the ruler is unrighteous, he will have little ability to lead people towards the path of dharma and will be unable to keep them from crime and dishonesty. As rulers become more and more crooked, the same character will naturally trickle down to the general populace. This illustrates why inefficiency and corruption are so rampant today.

3. Education must take responsibility to create the ‘good’: The only way is to orient education so that it focuses on creating citizens and leaders who are established in practice of dharma, morals and righteous conduct. We need to have systems which can identify, select and train people to be committed to working for good of society. The chief duty of a nation is to ensure that it chooses only ideal and ethically upright and truthful persons as their elected-representatives and administrators; if a state is without proper administrators, it becomes weak and decays.

4. The characteristic of a ruler: According to Bheeshma the prime protector of the citizens is a Kshatriya, which literally means those who protect people from kshat or unhappiness. A Kshatriya is said to display the characteristics of bravery, courage, vigilance, charity, the ability to display his prowess, and not retreating from battle. He has the ultimate duty to protect all beings along with fostering the righteous, destroying those who are cruel, and not fleeing from the enemy. Only two things go with him to the other world: his merits and his sins. Therefore, the rulers and administrators should carefully and gradually, earn the merit of righteousness.

A ruler should essentially be a man of action, of karma-yoga, not of bhoga. While destiny has a part and is powerful, the action is more powerful. Without action, a ruler can never help destiny to play its part. Both are potent, but the action is the more potent and it is the action which shapes destiny.

The next equally important duty of a ruler is to inspire confidence in the minds of people and to always be truthful. His behaviour should be above reproach. During education and training focus must be on ensuring that the rulers imbibe the characteristics of Self-restraint, righteousness, humility and qualities which a ruler needs to be successful. To have his passions under perfect control, should be the result of his schooling and should be his second nature. He should know the art of choosing his subordinates. He should have compassion as part of his mental make-up, but he should guard against too forgiving nature. He should know how not to reveal his plans and its weaknesses and should be a master of Dandaniti, capable of swift reward or punishment.

5. The making of Chanakya: As a young boy of 7-years, already recognized as a genius, Vishnu, who later was renowned as Chanakya, comes with his family to Pataliputra, to attend a gathering of scholars; where his father is wishing for a post in the king’s durbar. Early in childhood, he recognizes that nyaya , justice, was an absent master in Pataliputra. He had observed horrific injustices, innocent men pleading for nyaya being brutally executed without travesty of trial. He saw that injustice wore the garb of nyaya . He decided to stay, learn and observe. He lived a second life, which none could see, no spy could see what was within his young, genius, compassionate mind; and inside his mind-space, he built a new self, focused on one area, justice.

He was well aware and accepted that every Rama in this world will have a Ravana and as long as kindness exists its nemesis shall exist, too. It was the way of the world and dharma, justice, has to battle continuously with adharma . He couldn’t or didn’t protest against adharma he saw since he was young and realized that throwing filth in a dirty street is of no avail; filth invites filth. If a street is clean, adharma will be easily observed and so even the wicked will be cautious.

Within his mind, Vishnu was now focused on a single goal. To use his genius to reshape Magadh empire into a just, dharmic empire, a place where Nyaya was respected and followed by all citizens and the king. So, he learnt all about justice, nyaya . What better way to study nyaya than to observe its opposite, anyaya in action. He observed, watched, heard, overheard, deduced, picked up, noted and thru’ various ways gathered all info on injustices happening.

He never spoke of this aloud. His goal was not to save a man here or there but at the right time, someday in distant future of many years, to put all his insights, genius, to get the power to ensure justice triumphing over adharma . Rama, Pandavas, Draupdi, had waited for many years to get justice and he was ready to wait for many years and be prepared.

However, mere months later came an opportunity. His sister had escaped being kidnapped thanks to a pick-pocket who was now jailed, not for being a thief, but for preventing her kidnapping and thereby depriving the money which could have been earned for her! What should or could he do was the question before him. And as a bold courageous lad he took a leap into the unknown to fight injustice! ‘ Anyaya’ he shouted at the king’s durbar. To even suggest it was high treason and penalty was immediate execution of the entire family! All soldiers, ministers, king himself were shocked; the Prime Minister whose protégé Vishnu was, became red with anger while the queens and the prince laughed at the lisping lad!

Very politely, with calm clear voice, which pleased even the king, Vishnu pleaded “you yourself have taught us to fight injustice, and so I submit the ‘thief’ is innocent and has been wrongly arrested by a king’s guards closest to PM himself. The King now wants to know why Vishnu is interested in the thief, and Vishnu says, because he’s my brother-in-law, which apparently was not so, perhaps yet, but was a satisfactory reason! His boldness and wit please the King and his favorite prince, though the PM is seething with anger. Vishnu then states certain indisputable facts, which he has come to know, to prove his point, without blaming the PM. The thief is freed, and Vishnu is now appointed by the king as a companion to the Prince!

Are our schooling and governance focused on creating enlightened chanakya-like youths and leaders like Bheeshma?
