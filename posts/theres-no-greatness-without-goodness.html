India to Bharat · Essay #218

There’s ‘no greatness without goodness’

1. Elections are once again upon us and relations between parties in power and those in opposition continue to deteriorate, instead of improving. Irrespective of parties, democracy has totally failed to lead society towards greatness; instead, it has led to galloping selfishness and widespread corruption. It should now be very clear that democracy without ‘good’ people at the helm, is bound to go on deteriorating, since the new breed of the educated, whatever be their status or work, leaders or the led, see learning and ‘vocation’ as a ‘business for profits’, not a means of service to society and nation. What guarantees have we that democracy or any of its reformed measures will last unless it is based on first developing the goodness in man?

2. Can Democracy make India as great as Bharat was? While democracy has taken roots in India, as it has elsewhere, our education, governance, and democratic processes have failed to ensure that elected representatives and administrators are men of character, integrity and wisdom to know what is ‘right’ and, strength and fearlessness, to walk the talk towards the long-term good; not just pander to vote banks for short-term solutions.

3. Should we continue only with 3R’s, IQ based education? Shouldn’t we demand the addition of character and nation-building education as envisioned by Vivekananda, Gandhiji and the seers of Bharat? Shouldn’t we educate the young to become the srestha, enlightened citizens and role models for society willing to cooperate and work together to find long-term solutions for the greatest good of greatest numbers? Shouldn’t we educate them to be committed to their duties, to excellence, to seva-bhav, to the Fundamental Duties of our Constitution?

4. Not ‘Opposition’, but parties, leaders, members of ‘Cooperation’ are needed in Democracy: Once elected, the attempt of parties cannot be to prove that we are ‘right’ and you are wrong based on numbers and votes; but for all elected members to discuss and arrive at what is ‘Right’ for the long-term good of the nation; and, for this we must listen and learn from elders, from men of wisdom of all parties, and make decisions based on long-term good for Bharat not for self or party for immediate gains. If democracy is good for countries, shouldn’t it be so, for Political parties also! Is issuing a ‘whip’ which every party member has to follow, fair to democracy?

Only enlightened leaders can inspire everyone to work together for the good of the nation. Cooperation is better than the competition; abusing each other and fault finding is not the way. We don’t need political parties to be adversaries; we need educated leaders who are trustworthy and who, once elected, have the wisdom to sit and work together to make India a leader in world affairs once again, not cater to vote-banks – Leaders are to Lead not be led by Vote-banks.

5. The Paradox of Democracy: Democracy is based on the false premise and assumption that people are equal when we know for a fact that people are unequal. And, it is well said, never treat unequal people equally or equal people unequally! We know for a fact that everyone is not fit to lead, though many may want to enjoy its power, status, and money, and we see that Democracy helps and allows many such people to come to power.

Only those who have been working in a constituency should be allowed to contest; for they know the issues and people know of them! If unknown candidates are foisted to ‘fight’ elections at the last minute, they can promise the moon; a lot of marketing money is needed; and, people know him not, so how can they decide rightly!

In democracy ‘win-ability’ is the only consideration, not character and competence! And so, party-tickets are offered to people with loads of money or who have muscle-power to browbeat the people or because of caste equations in their constituency. Parties and the elected have a narrow, self-centred short-term vision of 3-5 years. They come and go. And, therefore they spend their energies in acquiring enough resources and goodwill by appeasing their vote banks, to get money to fight the next elections; naturally, they are obligated to help those who provided the resources and all this prompts people to attempt to recoup the money through corrupt, illegitimate means.

6. Education must enable us ‘know’ and ‘do’ the ‘right’: Democracy believes that people know what is good for them; the reality is that most of us look for immediate short-term pleasures and how to acquire the resources to fulfill them. Without any education or training to know the higher dimensions of work and life, it is not easy to decide what is good. Even Supreme Court judges, best trained to decide, having a written Constitution, give differing judgments as per own prejudices!

7. Building Viveka: The ability to ‘choose’ and ‘act’ upon the long-term good over immediate gratification, viveka, has to be acquired over a long period of time, effort, and experience, through continuous self-development practices. Only selfless persons with viveka are likely to work for the good of others and it is stupidity to expect the self-centered to be really interested in society’s well-being!

8. The wisdom of Bharat: Schooling must first ensure that the young get established in self-development practices to imbibe ethics, values, discipline, righteous conduct. Then they must choose a vocation which is their passion and by which they can best serve society. And, only then let them earn and enjoy so as to remain on the path towards ‘perfection’, peace, happiness, fulfillment, success and prosperity.
