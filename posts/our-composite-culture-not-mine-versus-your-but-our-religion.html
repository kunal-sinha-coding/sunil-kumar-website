India to Bharat · Essay #165

Our composite culture – Not “mine-versus-your” but our religion

1. According to the Fundamental Duties of our Constitution inter alia, it shall be the duty of every citizen to cherish and follow the noble ideas which inspired our national struggle for freedom; to promote harmony and the spirit of common brotherhood amongst all people; to value and preserve the rich heritage of our composite culture; to strive towards excellence in all spheres of individual and collective activity.

Our composite culture is not of my-versus-your religion but of OUR religion, described as Sanatana Dharma, universal and eternal virtues , goodness, fulfilment of duties, righteous conduct, following which all humanity may attain the utopia of the highest happiness and perfection, following their respective religious path, scriptures, self-development practices like prayers, worship, Yoga, etc. and universal human values.

2. Origin of Virtues: Virtues and goodness have to be universal and eternal; they cannot be claimed to have originated from any one Master, Prophet, God-man or Religion at any one particular period of time for a particular community and not for others! Goodness is self-evident and not dependent on the authority of any holy scripture, saint, religion, person or persons. Goodness is based on universal Truths and eternal principles relating to Man-to-Man; Man-to-Nature; Man-to-Nature-to-God.

We intuitively do know that goodness is good and certainly do try, though we often fail, to be good at least with those whom we consider ‘mine’; we don’t need to be told that hurting others is bad and being good to others is good! Our problem is not only of ‘ not-knowing ’ but of inability to manage our mind and desires; not being able to control ourselves from doing wrong!

3. Constant Practice: Knowing of virtues and goodness is easy! The challenge is being able to restrain our tendencies to be self-centered and then building the ability to do good in spite of our tendencies! This ability, powers of mind, to ‘restrain from doing bad’ and, to feel ‘others’ also as ‘ours’; has to be first learnt, understood intellectually, and only thru’ life-long continuous practice and awakening our powers of discrimination we learn to restrain ourselves from doing bad and learn to feel others also as ‘not they’, but ‘ours’. Different religions in their own way help us to struggle from selfishness to unselfishness; less and less of self and more and more of selflessness. The aim of religions is to inspire us to grow in the feelings of love, care, concern for each other till we actually experience fulfilment by feeling one with the whole creation.

4. Wisdom is not the property of any one Prophet or Religion; it is revealed to a galaxy of pure, selfless men of wisdom, aka Masters, Prophets, God-men, Incarnation, Seer, Rishis, who have realized the highest Truth and therefore have the holistic vision and can inspire us to be good. It is the common duty of religions to inspire and show us how to struggle continuously to become good, better, best. Followers of selfless saints gradually limit and bind the idea of goodness into a narrow cage of “My Religion, holy scriptures, life and teachings, mythology, etc.” vs “Yours”, which often degenerates into communal disharmony!

5. In India that is Bharat, the universal, eternal wisdom is termed as the Vedas, literally ‘knowledge’; and implying knowledge of the unseen; imperceivable by our sense organs. And the essence of this knowledge is contained in the Upanishads. These are the most ancient compilations of wisdom in the whole world, before modern religions and civilizations have emerged. Thus, they’re the common heritage of all mankind; the very foundation of our global composite culture. This universal wisdom was scattered broadcast for the first time, in the modern Western world and civilization, in Sept. 1893 by Vivekananda at the World Parliament of Religions, Chicago and the idea of harmony of religions, received worldwide acceptance. This most ancient wisdom is today expressed and reflected in the life and teachings of modern saints like Ramakrishna, Vivekananda, Aurobindo, Gandhiji, and many others who are now globalizing this universal message of wisdom, for all mankind.

6. Our composite culture holds that the purpose of any religion is to inspire and establish people in self-development practices of virtuous conduct, prayers, etc., to build powers and purity of mind to reach the goal of life. Vivekananda has expressed our composite culture in the following words: Each soul is potentially divine. The goal is to manifest this divinity within by controlling nature, external and internal. Do this either by Work, or Worship, or Meditation/Psychic control, or Knowledge/Philosophy; by one, or more, or all of these, and be free’; this is Religion; all other rituals, etc. are but secondary details. Just as Religion means ‘regain’, Yoga, means ‘union’ with our inherently blissful divine Self; Kingdom of God within; Know Thyself; Love thy neighbour as thy Self. Be you Perfect. Salvation, Nirvana, Heaven, Freedom, Perfection, etc. are expressions of the utopia of the ultimate goal of life, Bliss, Ananda.

7. Conclusion: Academia and religions have to come together to take the responsibility of inspiring us to imbibe the universal spirit of love for the whole creation by regular self-development practices so that we may strive to manifest our unlimited divine potential by working for the good of the universe, following our respective religion and self-development practices, that suit us.
