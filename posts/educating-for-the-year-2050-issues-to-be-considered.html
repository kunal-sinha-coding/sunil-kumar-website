India to Bharat · Essay #178

Educating for the year 2050 – Issues to be considered

Envisioning the year 2050: With the rapid rate at which technology is progressing, we’ve little idea of what the world will be like in 2050 or 2150; what will be the impact of the big issues like environment degradation, climate change, shortage of water, etc. How will super computers and robots change the meaning of being human? How will we deal with the epidemic of fake news? Will nations and religions still be relevant? Will there be death still? Is death the very end? And if it were so wouldn’t life be purposeless?

Shouldn’t we also start teaching – How to keep learning? How to adapt to Change? It is already clear that skills of memorizing, computing, writing, learning different languages, medical, legal counseling, etc. may not be needed by the masses. The idea of small shops and shopkeepers; stationary buildings with rooms for residences, classes, offices itself may not be there. Today itself we’re overloaded with information, with knowledge, with methods of entertainment, video-games, etc., whether needed, irrelevant, fake, or mischievous. Thinkers like Harari have given their ideas on major forces that shape societies and are likely to influence the story of our global future, in ‘21 Lessons for the 21st Century’ on topics like Disillusionment; Work; Liberty; Equality; Community;

Civilization; Nationalism; Religion; Terrorism; Humility; God; Secularism; Education; etc. He gives a probing and visionary view as we move into the uncharted territory of the future. As technology advances faster than our understanding of it, hacking becomes a tactic of war, and the world feels more polarized than ever, Harari addresses the challenge of navigating life in the face of constant and disorienting change and raises the important questions we need to ask ourselves in order to survive. What will the future workforce look like, and how should we ready ourselves for it? How should we deal with the threat of terrorism? Why is liberal democracy in crisis? He invites us to consider values, meaning, and personal engagement in a world of uncertainty.

Much more than IQ we will need a supple Mind: What we really need to teach today are the time-tested ability to know what is really important and, to be able to focus and concentrate on them. At the same time we also need to learn the ability to detach from the outdated irrelevant and refocus on the emerging needs. And, as things will be rapidly changing and change is always stressful, we will need to build a mind that would be creative, innovative and will have the ability to adjust, adapt and accommodate to the changing situations, and be willing to keep learning and de-learning. So far, the change was gradual and we spent our life in learning, working, enjoying, retiring, dying. In future the change will become more rapid and so, we’ve to constantly keep learning and coping with Artificial Intelligence, Robots, new vocations and skills, gadgets galore, new dating-marriage-lifestyles, etc.

And, to keep pace with changes, we would need not only a very powerful, flexible and supple mind; but also, much more clarity on the nature of our mind and how to manage our thoughts and keep it focused on the goal and purpose of my being. The age-old questions of who I am, where am I headed, what is the purpose of being a human being, etc. will be very much more important.

Not just 3R’ and IQ, but future generations will have to have much more of emotional maturity and strength as well as spiritual intelligence, EQ and SQ, to be able to cope with pace of change in life styles; and these cannot be learnt from bookish learning. They’re to be cultivated through regular and daily self-development practices. And so, we will need teachers, parents and elders who will all be trained to be mentors so that they can not only show how, but also inspire.

And, we would need a Holistic Worldview: With the information explosion and the universe becoming like a global village, the narrow narrative of religions will no longer remain bound in any particular religious dogmas and only the universal ideas and holistic worldview which appear scientific, logical and rational, will ultimately prevail.

Presumably and most certainly death shall remain and integral part of life. And, since the very idea of dying makes all science and technology seem irrelevant, the best scientists would be engaged in seeking to look beyond death. If there has to be a soul after death, as many religions assert, many other questions arise. What happens to the soul after death? Where does it go? What does it do there? How and why it takes birth? Many believe and intuitively sense and agree that we’re somehow connected to an undying soul! Some believe that we’ve an eternally present soul and there is a Supreme Soul with which we all are connected. Hypnotherapists like M Newton are already discovering answers to many of these issues and through case studies have detailed them in ‘Journey of Souls’. During hypnosis the subjects movingly describe what has happened to them between their former reincarnations on earth. They reveal graphic details about how it feels to die, who meets us right after death, what the spirit world is really like, where we go and what we do as souls, and why we choose to come back in certain bodies!

And so, it seems that the more we go into the technological world of the future, the more we come back to the age-old basic human questions which the Vedic Seers and the Greek philosophers asked and in answering which, we have created numerous religions: Who am I? Where am I going? How to get there? It is now for the academia and scientists to give us answers which all can perhaps agree upon!
