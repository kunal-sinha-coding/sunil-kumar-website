India to Bharat · Essay #201

Dialogue between Mr. Bharat and Mr. India on this International Yoga Day

1. Will 3R’s modern education give us happiness? Mr. India has the faith that mere 3R’s secular education, science and technology will solve all our problems and give us peace and happiness. He questions: Can religion help in solving our economic and social problems?

2. Bharat asks: What is the purpose of Religion? And, explains that the purpose of all Religions is to inspire us to be good and better towards the highest and best, in and thru’ our daily work and life. It is only by practicing basic, eternal, universal moral and ethical principles, i.e. “Sanatana Dharma”, and imbibing them in our character that we can remain on the critical path towards the highest human goal of perpetual peace and happiness, i.e. an ideal of “perfection”.

‘Religion is simply a process of striving to lead an ethical life which purifies our mind and thereby we can manifest more and more of our omnipotent potential, i.e. all the power, goodness, virtues, the Divinity, already in man, as Life and Consciousness.

Not ‘My’ versus ‘Your’ Religion but leading an ethical life is Bharat’s idea of religion: Mr. Bharat now critically compares and highlights some of the divisive features of modern religions, which are dominating the worldview of today:

They are exclusive and dogmatic, based on one Prophet and one scripture.

They claim that their scripture alone is true and their God as the only true God.

Their exhortations are to their own religious communities, often as against others.

T hey demand and enforce social and religious conformity

They often preach even active hostility towards the so-called non-believers (Kafirs, heathens)

They believe that man has only one life in this world and it is purely by chance or God’s arbitrary will that one is born able or disabled, rich or poor and one will be awarded eternal hell or heaven according to his deeds.

Salvation also is exclusive and is dependent largely on one’s being a ‘believer’ in the dogmas and creeds of that religion and in their God. It is not for all but only for those who are ‘believers’ of their faith.

Salvation is not just dependent on virtuous and spiritual living, however pure and noble and morality is tied up more with formal religious beliefs than on universal values

Immoral and cruel behavior towards non-believers is quite common.

They aim at increasing their numbers through conversions for political power

They hold that creation had a beginning roughly about 6000 years ago and will end.

They hold that man is a born sinner; primarily he is a body, in which a created soul has been breathed.

4. Bharat’s Vision of Man – Each soul is potentially divine: In our busy life we forget the same profound Biblical message: The Kingdom of God is within you. Seek you first the kingdom of God and his righteousness and everything else will be added unto you. Similarly, Vivekananda says:

The Background, the Reality, of everyone is that same Eternal, Ever Blessed, Ever Pure, and Ever Perfect One. It is the Atman, the Soul, in the saint and the sinner, in the happy and the miserable, in the beautiful and the ugly, in men and in animals; it is the same throughout. It is the Shining One.

Religion is realization; not talks, not doctrine not theories, however beautiful they may be. It is being and becoming, not hearing or acknowledging; it is the whole soul becoming changed into what it believes.

Doing work is not religion, but work done rightly leads to freedom.

Religion comes from intense self-sacrifice. Desire nothing for yourself. Do all for others. This is to live and move and have your being in God.

The difference between God and the devil is in nothing except in unselfishness and selfishness.

It is a privilege to serve mankind, for this is the worship of God; God is here, in all these human souls. He is the soul of man’.

5. Profile of a modern man, an Indian: Bharat tries to draw up a general profile of a modern man, the educated Indian of today, who has governed us and decided what an educated Indian should be schooled in!

He is generally well meaning, attempts to live a good life and to the extent he succeeds, he is more or less content. He may raise a family, pursue a career, work on the land, in an office, factory, or do his own work or business. He may be rich, talented, famous or poor.

Whatever or wherever he is, his desires are endless: He desires love, wealth, power, health, esteem, fame and all pleasures he can afford. If lucky he enjoys many and if unlucky, he gets very little. In either case his fears and angers are as many as his desires, for fear and anger are the other side of desire

He fears failure, aloneness, loss of status, fame, health, wealth, poverty, and above all he hates death. His attitudes, worldview and reactions are governed by his desires and fears, sometimes to a neurotic and destructive degree but more often to a degree that is considered normal for any ordinary person of today.

6. Mr. Bharat highlights the Purposelessness of a Mr. India: The modern man is thoroughly imbued with a concept of his limitations in this vast and uncertain world.

When he thinks about it, he finds himself to be a tiny bit of living matter, fragile, perishable, prey to hostile forces, or forces beyond his control. Though he enjoys the comforts of modern technology as well as his own smartness, he is fundamentally limited and ignorant of the higher dimensions of education, work and life. He does not know the purpose of his life or the purpose of creation as a whole. He does not know the future and conveniently forgets the past.

His powers of reasoning though generally adequate for his daily life, cannot help him be in a state of happiness, peace, bliss, total fulfillment – sukha, shanti, ananda, poornatva, which he really seeks. This limited body-mind is his entire being and while he can appreciate its relative insignificance, he finds it all-important. No love, no attachment is greater than the fear that it may be destroyed, though indeed, it will. He does enjoy some or many or hardly any of modern comforts, but he is always hoping for better times, and is often under tremendous stress, he worries for his job, or promotion, or just to earn his living.

7. Bharat questions India: Why have you accepted the divisive worldview of ‘Separateness’ and focus on the ‘Objective’ external world, of the West? Why have you not learnt from your Bharatiya forefathers that Religion literally implies to ‘regain’ our lost peace and happiness just as Yoga literally implies being ‘united’ with our natural state of peace and happiness. Since we all wish for peace and happiness why should schooling not make this their focus?

It is because you Mr India who are governing us today, are totally unaware of the wisdom of ‘oneness’ of Bharat that you have fallen for the narrow, selfish, petty-minded, divisive modern Western idea of ‘My-Religion, my-God, my-Scripture’ versus ‘your inferior religion-God-Scripture’.

8. Conclusion : While we celebrate Yoga-day, let us remember that yoga is not just exercises. Vivekananda whose Birthday is celebrated as National Youth Day has popularized the ideals of Karma yoga, Bhakti yoga, Gyana Yoga, Raja Yoga, which must become an integral part of our schooling so that all mankind can work together to become good and better towards the best.
