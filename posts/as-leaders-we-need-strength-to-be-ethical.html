India to Bharat · Essay #187

As leaders we need strength to be ethical

Purpose of all religions: Shorn of all wrappings of rituals, mythology, life, teachings; essentially all religions give us an ideal of “perfection” to be attained; the ultimate aim and purpose of life. Religions then inspire us to follow some spiritual practices, ie self-development processes, eg prayers, meditation, worship, self-study, etc to develop the strength and ability to walk-the-talk of being ethical and virtuous to attain the goal of “perfection”.

This “Perfection” cannot be realised and attained by the weak! It needs tremendous struggle and strength to overcome our selfishness and other negative tendencies and material attachments to be on the righteous ethical path to perfection. Unfortunately, schooling provides no opportunity to discuss, explore, reflect, on the profound ideas which are the very foundation of the composite culture of India that is Bharat.

Is it important for us to know the message of strength of Religions? Shouldn’t schooling give us an opportunity to hear of the highest truth, reflect upon it, and become strong and capable of walking-the-talk to experiencing our oneness with the whole creation and be totally-fulfilled? Shouldn’t we teach the essence of all religions? Of oneness, of the unity of existence, of the whole world as our family, and exhort them to struggle and stop not till the highest Perfection is attained? In absence of academic rigour, preachers of religions weaken, divide, cause communal disharmony, instead of inspiring us to be strong and cooperative.

Solution? Our challenge is to take help from Yoga teachers, counsellors, and spiritually evolved volunteers of all religions and faiths to strategize how we can inspire students to struggle to build bahu-bal, buddhi-bal, atma-bal; physical-strength, intellectual-strength and Strength of character and virtues that flow from the Self, the potential within.

‘This Atman cannot be attained by the weak’ , is a profound statement of Vedanta, of the Upanishads which are a mine of strength, the foundation of our composite culture, and the motto of a renowned school. The Fundamental Duties, Code of Ethics of National Council of Teacher Education, and the Supreme Court, etc. all have directed that the strength of character, the very foundation of our composite culture be taught. Why? Because we need strength to be ethical – only cowards lie and cheat.

Unfortunately, we the educated have no courage of conviction to ensure that character-strength alone should be the end-product of schooling and entry-point for leadership in political and governance, not marks and degrees.

The challenge: Today our leadership has become corrupt, selfish, narrow-minded and no longer inspires the young to be ethical and in fact compels them to be unethical! Since we have no role models, it is certainly necessary to give the young the opportunity to get an intellectual conviction in power of virtues and character and inspire them to build the strength of mind, will power, self-restraint, virtuous living. Schooling must, therefore, provide time and space for self-study, reflection and self-development practices. Otherwise, we get knowledge but not wisdom and strength of character! Righteous ethical conduct, straight-forwardness, human values, originate from strength.

And what is the source of strength, source of potential? It is the unchanging Atman, the Self. And how do we draw strength from the Atman? By learning to gradually learn to identify more and more with the unchanging Atman and less and less with the ever-changing mind and selfish-ego. How?

By Service to God in man: When we serve jivas with the idea that he is a jiva, it is daya, compassion, but not Prema, love. But when we serve jiva with the idea that he is, in essence, the Atman, seeing God in them, it is Prema. It is said “Love God” but “Have compassion for jivas”. However, from Vedantic point of view, Jiva-atman and Param-atman are the same in essence and so our principle should be to serve living being seeing God in them; not just service out of pity or compassion. Who am I to pity others! Not mere daya but Worship of God-in-man is and should be our ideal to be learnt and practiced. In the bygone era the highest men in India were busy seeking God in their meditations and had no concern for society other than to teach the principles of Vedanta thru’ scholarly discussions to the very few; who then communicated the ideals to society thru’ epics like Ramayana, Mahabharata, etc. It was ok then, because society was in general Self-managed thru’ the ‘vyavastha’ of varna-ashrama, schooling based on establishing the young in the ideals of Dharma and Swa-dharma thru’ daily sadhna and swadhyaya, etc. But today, Teachers and parents have to be mentors.

People tend to rise to levels beyond their character and competence , says Peters Principle, and today we’re its victims! It is very interesting to note from Abul Fazl’s, ‘Aini Akbari’ that His Majesty, Akbar believed that the Daroga “should be watchful to see that no one from covetousness abandons his own profession”! And he gives the example of the King of Persia who once forgot a verse. Thereupon his torchbearer quoted it. He punished the speaker somewhat, and said, ‘When a menial takes to learning he does so at the expense of his duties’!

Leadership: Is everyone fit for leadership? The ancients believed that leaders were born, not made; but this belief was challenged by the West when societies became more democratic. Now leaders had to be ‘found’, educated, trained for the Army, civil services, business and industries. Inheritance of leadership was now considered unjust! Well, it is obvious that leaders do have special traits and Leadership Qualities, LQ; but where do LQ come from? Why in some, not in others? No clarity! New studies are showing that LQ does come from birth, just as some have talent for music, etc. Do they come from genes, DNA, or are these the talents, characteristics, inclinations or samskars that we have ourselves acquired in some earlier unknown past? The latter view seems most logical to us.

Is schooling helping to build LQ in the educated who will be in leadership positions in all walks of life? Sadly, no. We as parents and teachers focus only on marks; we push people into positions beyond their character and competence and we suffer the consequences of self-centred leaders and systems, where no one seems responsible for results!

Conclusion: Teachers as mentors can help create enlightened citizens who can take leadership responsibility to work together as leaders of cooperation, not leaders of opposition, to solve problems of society, based on the work ideal of work as worship of God in man.
