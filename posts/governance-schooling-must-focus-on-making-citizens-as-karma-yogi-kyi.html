India to Bharat · Essay #098

Governance & schooling must focus on making citizens as Karma Yogi, KYi

The Idea and Ideal of being a KYi: During schooling and growing up every citizen, irrespective of any differences, must learn and become established in commitment to ethical and righteous conduct and all their various duties, including the Constitutional Fundamental Duties.

For achieving commitment to their respective ‘duties’, i.e., swadharma, schooling must help and inspire every citizen to get established in the habit of daily self-development practices to awaken their viveka-buddhi, i.e., powers of discrimination between ‘bad, good, better, best; and then strive to develop the powers of mind, strength and abilities to get committed to selflessly performing their prescribed duties to the best of their abilities and for the good of society as a whole, not just for ‘me and mine’.

This is the Idea and Ideal of being a KYi; and all systems of governance, working and living must be designed in a way that will help citizens to imbibe the spirit of being a Karma Yogi.

It is obvious that only when we practice and learn to manage our mind from a young age, can we acquire a healthy worldview of work and life and start realizing that selflessness is far superior to selfishness and will learn to practice remaining calm and composed, irrespective of success and failures, and will learn to focus on fulfilling our duties, not just on the ‘fruits’ of our actions.

Schooling and growing up is the best opportunity to learn and appreciate that our responsibility is to fulfil our duties as our highest worship, in the spirit of seva-bhav, selfless-service, for good of samaaja instead of working for material benefits and happiness, which of course will accrue as a bye product of our service to society.

If we believe in a super intelligence that is responsible for all those things for which you and we are not responsible for, aka a God who is One, Omnipresent, all pervasive, all seeing, all knowing, we can or may consider ‘knowing that mystery’ as God and as our common, highest and best Goal. Karma Yoga tells us how to be on the path to the Goal.

And since we’re all continuously engaged in some form of ‘work’, it could certainly be mutually beneficial if we learn how to ‘work’ (Karma Yoga, KY) in a way following the idea and ideal of a Karma Yogi, KYi, so that our vocation and daily work and interactions become an opportunity for knowing that highest Mystery, aka worship of ‘God’ as man and Creation!

Once we learn the importance of being committed to our respective duties and start fulfilling our duties, we will start realizing divinity in all beings and in everything; and will get the insight that reward-seeking actions are far inferior to works performed as worship without attachment to results of actions.

2. We all are aware that we’re continuously facing ethical dilemmas between ‘bad and worse’ and between ‘good, better, best’ etc. Life is a process of making choices and we’ve the freedom of choice. However, we become enslaved by our choices and actions. Arjuna faces such a crisis of conscience in the midst of the battlefield and admits that his mind has become very restless; that he is unable to cope with the current situation as his mind refuses to take part in such a battle, where he has to kill his elders and teachers! Fortunately, facing this ethical dilemma he asks for advice and guidance from a ‘shrestha’, his Mentor Krishna and requests him to be his spiritual teacher and guide him on the proper path of action – Karma Yoga so that the action may take him on and towards the path of good, better, best.

It is to be noted that at the very outset Krishna explains the idea and ideal of the ‘Best’ by pointing out first that a living-being is a ‘Jiva’+ ‘atma’. The Jiva, i.e., the self, consisting of the body-mind complex decays and perishes whereas the Atman, Spirit, Rooha, Self, of the nature of pure Singular Consciousness is immortal, eternal and imperishable. Death only destroys the physical body, but the subtle Mind-soul, enlivened by Consciousness continues its journey. Just as a person discards his old clothes and adorns new ones, the Mind-soul keeps changing bodies from one lifetime to another.

The Mentor then reminds Arjun that his ‘social’ responsibility, respective duty/Swadharma as an ‘administrator/protector’ of society is to ensure justice for the Righteous people of society by upholding righteousness.

He explains that performing one’s social duty is a virtuous action/karma. Karma is a holistic idea of action because it includes ‘external action’ as well as the ‘internal effect’ of action that are left as impressions, memories stored in our mind, which build-up our personality, character and enslaves us and make us what we’re at any moment, good or bad, based on the intentions of our actions.

He emphasizes that it is Arjuna’s duty to engage in this action which has come up to him as his respective duty and fulfilling his duty will take him on his respective path to that ideal of the highest and best, while dereliction will only lead to infamy and humiliation… Contd.
