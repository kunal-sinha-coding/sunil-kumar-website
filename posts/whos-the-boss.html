India to Bharat · Essay #228

Who’s the boss?

1. Good governance needs good leaders: The governance systems we have created remind us of the fable, ‘andher nagari chaupat raja…’. If things go wrong in existing systems, we are unable to swiftly identify and punish the wrong-doer, or reward the efficient. just as the raja of the fable was unable to fix responsibility.

2. In the present pyramid systems, people report to the ‘boss’ above and are not judged by whether the citizens have satisfaction! We make the ‘boss’ our ‘customer’ and ignore the citizens, the real ‘customer’ whom we are to serve.

3. We need leaders who can create good systems where the ‘result producers’ are clearly identifiable and have total responsibility for delivery of results. There need be no bosses but mentors, facilitators, helpers who ensure that the ‘result producers’ have all training, resources and help needed to deliver.

4. Issues for reflection: As Aristotle puts it, it is better to be governed by a good man than by good laws. People tend to do what they are evaluated upon. How do we develop and evaluate leadership and citizenship? What syllabus and action plan are we following to build it?

5. As elders of society we have to ask ourselves: In our modern concepts of politics, governance, education who is responsible and accountable for:

Developing the enlightened leaders and citizens who can create good systems for good governance?

Making citizens aware of and committed to the Fundamental Duties (Article 51A)?

Making citizens aware of and committed to kartavya-palan, to their duties to family, organization, society?

Inculcating in the citizen’s the spirit of striving for quality and excellence in work?

Inculcating in the citizen’s spontaneous feelings of love, care and concern for each other; human-values?

Thinking, planning, evolving, and implementing a shared vision of the future for the long-term good of individuals and society as a whole?

6. Don’t keep a dog and bark yourself! Delegation must be given to the lowest competent level and so, those who actually ‘deliver’ results, must be given full respect and dignity; they will then take responsibility and take pride in being accountable. Even a child when given responsibility tries his very best to succeed. It would have been our ‘result producers’, the workforce, who had made India great, mentored by their ‘teachers’; within systems monitored by ‘administrators’ and due to the entrepreneurship of the ‘businessmen’.

7. Seniors as mentors, not boss: The job of seniors, policymakers and executives, is to be facilitators; not of bossing and interfering in day-to-day issues. Seniors have to focus not on short-term issues but on long-term planning for future, for continuous improvements in all areas and systems.

Who will bell the cat!! Of course, individually and in groups, we can continue reflecting on ideas to identify and instil values for good governance.
