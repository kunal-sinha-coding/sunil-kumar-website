India to Bharat · Essay #056

A universal Gospel of Sanatana Dharma for all who are going towards the highest and best (2)

Continuing from the Introduction to Complete Works of Vivekananda by Sister Nivedita:

  • The glory of Hinduism comes from the Seer who promises: “Hear, you children of immortal bliss! Even you that dwell in higher spheres! For I have found that Ancient One who is beyond all darkness, all delusion. And knowing Him, you also shall be saved from death”
  • India is one with all faiths in her understanding that Progress is from the subtle to gross; from the ‘many’ to the ‘One’; from the low to the high; from the form to the formless
  • India differs from other religions, only in having a word of sympathy and promise for every sincere conviction, wherever and whatever it may be, as constituting a step in the great ascent to the highest human perfection.
  • Like great masters before him, Vivekananda stands merely as a Revealer, the interpreter to India of the treasures that she herself possesses in herself. The truth that he gives us would still be truths, even if he had never come! Our problem would have been our inability to access them thru the mammoth Veda in Sanskrit.
  • Vivekananda presents the truths in a concise and organized way to suit present milieu in inspiring English to a modern mindset seeped in Western culture, with which we the educated Indian elite are (unfortunately!) more familiar! Had he not come, these truths of divinity of man, oneness of all creation, work as worship, etc. would have remained as food for sastrarth, in scholarly disputes in forests.
  • He taught with authority for he had identified himself with the great Spirit within us all and so he spoke, not from ego-mind complex, as we, or as a Pundit would, but from the very Spirit, the Life or Consciousness already within us all! He scattered broadcast the highest truths to the pariah, the outcast and the foreigners, for which he was criticised by his Guru-bhais even!
  • He accepted Advaita as including that experience in which all is one, without a second but also accepted the ideals of Dvaita and Vishishtadvaita, as all these are leading to the same goal of Oneness.
  • He included these as a part of the still greater and simple doctrine that ‘the many and the One are the same Reality, perceived by the mind at different times and in different attitudes; echoing the actual experience of Ramakrishna that “God is both with form and without form. And He is that which includes both form and formlessness”
  • He creates a meeting-point between the East and the West and also of the past and future which leads to a profound idea: If the many and the One be indeed the same Reality, then it is not all modes of worship alone, but equally all modes of work, all modes of struggle, all modes of creation, which are paths to realization of the highest perfection. No distinction, henceforth, between the sacred and secular. To labour is to pray. Life is itself religion. This is the message which makes Vivekananda so relevant:
  • That our daily work, karma, is not as divorced from, but as expressing knowledge and devotion, Gyana and Bhakti, in our daily work and living.
  • That the factory, the study, the farm and the work of labour are as true and fit field for the meeting of God with man, as the temple or the monastery of a monk.
  • That there is no difference between service of man and worship of God, between manliness and faith, between true righteousness and spirituality.
  • Art, science, and religion are but three different ways of expressing a single truth; but in order to understand this we must have the idea of Oneness as Spirit, Advaita.
  • Threefold are the influences in the making of Vivekananda, first his literary Sanskrit and English education. Second the profound vision of ‘the whole world as our family’ which wasn’t stumbled upon just by accident, but our ancient seers had searched, logically analyzed like a scientist and recorded their experiences for posterity; and in the life of Ramakrishna he saw the vision of oneness of the Veda, lived and expressed by an illiterate person who had never read them but got the knowledge and burning desire for supreme knowledge, from his sincere worship of his Ishta-devata. Finally, he wandered all over India mixing with saints and scholars, simple souls, learning from all, teaching to all, living with all, seeing India as it was and now is.
  • These then, the Shastras, the Guru, and the motherland are the three notes that mingle to form the music of the gospel of Vivekananda, which he provides in the few years of work between 11th Sept. 1893 and 4th July, 1902.
  • And we are yet to realize and understand the vastness and significance of the message that he spoke.
