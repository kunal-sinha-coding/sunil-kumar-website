India to Bharat · Essay #191

The art and science of punishment for good governance

1. The Modern Confusion – Who will Punish Whom? The governance structure is such a complex network that no one knows who is responsible for what. We’ve the Courts where justice is blind, where there’re false witnesses and smart lawyers who seek not for truth, but to defend their respective clients. We’ve the governance with CBI, Vigilance, ladder of bosses, etc. etc. And, the result is that rich and smart crooks defended by richer and smarter lawyers escape punishment whereas poor, naive and innocents face great harassment. Inefficiencies and non-delivery of public services are well known; corruption, scams, loot of public money, bribery, etc. are rampant in every walk of life. It is also a fact that the very idea of democracy leads to a host of corrupt practices and if leadership itself doesn’t have the moral authority to punish, where is the likelihood of ensuring swift punishment and justice to people.

Today in democracy there is no clear-cut authority, responsibility and accountability and those in power come and go, and there in no one who has the supreme decision-making authority like the erstwhile idea of a king, the ‘ruler’, who was there for a long-term and who could be educated to strive to be good, better, best.

2. How our Sastras discuss the principles of Punishment: Sastras (shasan+trayate), literally mean a code of conduct and ethics, i.e. rules, regulations, instructions, that can protect us from going wrong. It is impersonal to the individual and that gives it authority, but at the same time it is personal to the collective as it is based on their time-tested experience and wisdom. Let’s now see how our ancient culture talks of Punishment:

Punishment is the protector of all creatures. Punishment is to be inflicted justly and swiftly and it alone is good governance, watching over people while they sleep. Only if it is properly inflicted after due consideration, does Punishment make people prosperous and happy. If it is given without due consideration, it destroys everything.

Without punishing the guilty, the stronger would bully the weaker. The whole creation is kept in order by ‘danda-niti’, fear of punishment, for a guiltless man is rare indeed!

3. Who can Punish? He who delivers justice should discern well. The Lord created his own son, Punishment, as justice, for the Ruler. That Ruler can be a fair and a just inflictor of punishment who is truthful, who has awakened Viveka, who is a man of wisdom. He knows the respective value of ‘ethics, wealth, pleasure, Perfection’, i.e. dharma, artha, kaama, mukti, the purusartha or striving, for the highest human goal of all mankind.

The ruler has to be a role model of highest self-discipline, continuously seeking mental purity: ‘Day and night he must strenuously exert himself to conquer his senses, for he alone who has self-control, can be fair and just and can inspire obedience in others’. The ruler is expected to be proficient in governance, and have mastery over the principles of various professions and trades. And, above all a ruler must have the ‘supreme knowledge of our potential divinity’. Thus, this supreme knowledge attainable thru’ daily self-development practices, is expected both from the acharya and the ruler.

Only a ruler who is of pure mind, faithful to his promises, who acts according to the codes of conduct and ethics, who has good assistants and is wise, has the moral courage to punish swiftly.

4. Who is unfit to Punish? Punishment is hard to be administered by people with impure mind who are not striving for the highest human goal of Perfection, in and thru’ their daily work and living. We may not have ‘rulers’ today but the question is whether those now in authority in governance have the moral authority and purity of character, to punish? And, we know that modern schooling is not at all concerned with building character!

4.1 Now we can appreciate why our administrators and political leaders are unable to deliver swift justice and punishment and provide efficient and effective governance! As we’ve seen mere IQ, educational qualifications and positional authority are not sufficient. The problem is that those in power apparently don’t seem to be having the moral authority to set up efficient systems to be able to identify and swiftly punish the guilty. Without the fear of punishment, we all know how uncaring, corrupt and inefficient are our organizations and they just don’t deliver desired results for the good of citizens.

4.2 It must be noted that IQ, Knowledge, Skills can be imparted to all and sundry as modern education is doing; but it is not at all easy to impart, inculcate, imbibe values and character. While a man of character can always be educated and trained to be a top performer and lead governance, the reverse is not possible. It is only men of character who can establish and maintain strong societal and institutional codes of ethics. While today we do create top performers, we’ve failed to train and create educational and recruitment systems which can find enough leaders with character, the Shrestha, who will be willing to strive totally unselfishly, in a spirit of ‘tyaga’ and ‘seva’, for the good of society.

4.3 We can now appreciate that authority and power of designations, lose their effectiveness in absence of personal self-lessness, character and moral authority. It is this personal authority which the defense forces try to instill but is missing from the political leadership and governance. And, that is why Bharat has the ideal, not of a leader with IQ and skills, but the idea of a selfless person, a Shrestha, an Acharya, men of righteous conduct, of simple living and high thinking. Only such men are really fit to occupy positions of a ‘ruler’, and can be trusted to provide just punishment.

5. Focus on Quality not just Quantity! Throughout schooling we’re focused on quantity in terms of marks, money, status, things; not on quality. Quality is an inner resolve to do one’s job, no matter how routine and rule bound it may seem, and do it better each succeeding day. It is not doing more of the same thing in a shorter time, but rather to impart to each element of a job an intangible aura, a perfection, so that anyone who sees it would know at a glance that this job was done by someone who is in the right vocation. Every piece of our job should glow with the inner illumination that it is the result of a labor of love. Yoga is skill in work, skill of being able to detach from results, so as to be totally focused on task and to punish swiftly and justly. When we love our work, we need not be managed by hopes of rewards or fears of punishment. Unfortunately, our systems are based on mistrust and so, we’ve been focused on checks and controls of people who want to beat or exploit systems for personal greed.

6. Our self-centered leaders have failed to inspire us to give our best: Human behavior is goal oriented and so, modern systems try to motivate people thru’ fear of rewards and punishment whereas Indian insights focus not on motivating, but on inspiring us to do our best, for that alone is our highest good. This spirit of striving for quality in work and decision-making and giving our best has to be inculcated during schooling by establishing us in daily self-development practices. Schooling must give us a lofty vision, the higher the better and inspire us to strive to be men of character, which comes first and the rest follows.

If we’re given a healthy worldview of our potential divinity, the goal of life and how our vocation and work can help us realize our divine potential then we will get an intellectual conviction in the power of goodness and struggle for the higher.
