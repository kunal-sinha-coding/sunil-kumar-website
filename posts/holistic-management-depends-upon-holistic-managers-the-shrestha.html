India to Bharat · Essay #166

Holistic management depends upon holistic managers, the Shrestha

Our Role in Creation – striving to be a Shrestha: As we’ve seen except us, everything in creation is willy-nilly adhering to their respective duties, swadharma . We have been given buddhi to fulfill our desires. If education and training programs focus on giving us a holistic vision and in developing our Viveka-buddhi during the many years in schools, colleges and at work, we will learn that only by fulfilling our swadharma can we be healthy, happy, successful at work and in life.

A manager as a Shrestha strives to understand the idea of the whole creation and our interconnectedness with it, thru’ our daily work and life. He is learning to choose the path of fulfilment of duties, ethical conduct, selflessness, over being negligent, unethical, selfish. He has the inspiration to struggle to be a better manager of our daily work and life.

Becoming a Shrestha, a holistic manager: Along with all knowledge and skills needed for a manager, a holistic manager is also focused on continuous daily self-development practices ( sadhna ), so as to understand and grow towards the healthy, holistic worldview of ‘Oneness’ and build emotional stability to traverse the critical path towards feelings of love, care and concern for all. This awakens the ‘right brain’, the intuitive and holistic part, and enables the holistic manager to quickly adjust and adapt to changes and makes him more creative and innovative in his pursuit of quality and continuous improvements. Daily self-development practices are essential for a Shrestha and would include daily Quiet time for self-study ( swadhyaya ) for acquiring conviction in the goal of life, critical path to it, and the role of work in achieving it.

Team Spirit: A holistic manager becomes an inspirational leader for team work, trust building and interpersonal relations and empowers teams to assume responsibility both for results and continuous improvements. A Shrestha is a role model of excellence both in work externally and, in character, internally. He is a role model of punctuality, time keeping on work assigned, norms of good behavior and work output and quality. He is personally committed to daily sadhna and swadhyaya, and treats work as worship for good of society.

Uniqueness of a holistic manager: The Shrestha reflects: Is the purpose of business just to make money or is it to satisfy the needs of society? Can we not consider our work and businesses as an opportunity to serve? Is peace, happiness, harmony not as important as money? Can I enjoy my money by causing unhappiness and poverty? Is selfishness better than enlightened self-interest? Can we get best out of people by ‘labelling’ them, e.g. Sweeper, Peon, Clerk, Departmental Manager, Worker, etc.? Aren’t they all Owners/CEO/Managers of their home, where they manage so many things? Isn’t a house wife managing the whole house hold without any management degree? Doesn’t the illiterate paan-wallah, street vendor, etc. manage their whole business?

Who is the Owner of a factory or even our cars? Once we hand over the charge to the managers and workers or to the driver of the car, don’t they become the ‘owners’ in practical terms? Do we treat the employees as the owner? If we give them respect, responsibility, autonomy, they will rise to the occasion.

A Holistic Manager understands that man is Jiva+atma, created in the image of God, and is quite capable of managing many things including his whole life. Even a 5-year-old child, given the responsibility rises, and likes to be able to learn to be responsible. So, is it necessary for a worker and a producer of results to have so many layers of Supervisors? Why should the customer pay for all these layers of watch dogs?

A Shrestha recognizes that each employee has infinite, divine potential within himself and that along with their spouse they manage their organization, the household, effectively and manage finance, purchase, human resource development, training, relationship, leadership, motivation, etc. And so, he empowers the employees to form Self-managing teams and take responsibility to produce results. By making the result-producer teams directly responsible and accountable for results and payments linked to it, they feel empowered-owners for their piece of work? This has been our traditional wisdom.

Mere managers ‘buy’ work; so, they want more work for less money! This is a model of conflict! The holistic or Viveka manger draws his inspiration from his mother and parents; even from mother earth and the entire creation. Mother gives love and all that we need, food, nurturing, strength, emotional support, autonomy to stand on our own feet; all out of love, without any return. Similarly, the father gives discipline and advice, whereas the grandparents give autonomy and wisdom. So, we do respond and try and give our best in return.

Work for good of society: This is our traditional wisdom of work as Yagna; by nourishing and cooperating with each other and being in ‘Sur’, in harmony with creation, we shall all attain the highest. So, instead of boss-management the holistic manager promotes and empowers the concept of self-managed teams and reminds them to take total responsibility for results, just as they take in their home. The Unions were supposed to unite management and workers, but today they oppose each other; a holistic manager builds trust and so the whole organization learns to work and grow together to satisfy the needs of society; not to arouse greed.

Management vs Holistic Management: Worldview of ‘separateness’ TO ‘Oneness’; Focus on ‘external’ factors TO ‘internal’ peace also; Managing, Motivating, Leadership over others TO Self-management, Self-inspiration, striving to be, and to inspire team to be Shrestha.
