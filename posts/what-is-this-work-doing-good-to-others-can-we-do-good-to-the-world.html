India to Bharat · Essay #185

What is this work? Doing Good to others? Can we do good to the world?

For all who work, Ethics-in-work, Karma-Yoga is the method to attain the Highest Freedom, Ananda. We need not believe in any doctrine, Religion or even in God, if it so suits us. We may just aim to solve the mystery of work and life, by striving for quality and excellence in work, in our vocation. Buddha said, I do not care to know your various theories about God. What is the use of discussing all the subtle doctrines about the soul? Do good and be good. And this will take you to freedom and to whatever truth there is.

What is this work? Doing Good to others? Can we do good to the world? In an absolute sense, no we cannot do good to the world; in a relative sense, yes. No permanent or everlasting good can be done to the world; if it could be done, the world would not be this world, says Vivekananda.

We may satisfy the hunger of a man for some time, but he will be hungry again. Every pleasure may be seen to be momentary. No one can permanently cure this ever-recurring fever of pleasure and pain. Can any permanent happiness be given to the world?

In the ocean we cannot raise a wave without causing a hollow somewhere else. The sum total of the good things in the world has been the same throughout in its relation to man’s need and greed. It cannot be increased or decreased. Take the history of the human race as we know it today. Do we not find the same miseries and the same happiness, the same pleasures and pains, the same differences in position?

Are not some rich, some poor, some high, some low, some healthy, some unhealthy? All this was just the same with the Egyptians, the Greeks, and the Romans in ancient times as it is with the modern world today. So far as history is known, it has always been the same; yet at the same time we find that, running along with all these incurable differences of pleasure and pain, there has ever been the struggle to alleviate them. Every period of history has given birth to thousands of men and women who have worked hard to smooth the passage of life for others.

And how far have they succeeded? We can only play at driving the ball from one place to another. We take away pain from the physical plane, and it goes to the mental one. It is like that picture in Dante’s hell where the misers were given a mass of gold to roll up a hill. Every time they rolled it up a little, it again rolled down. All our talks about a golden age are very nice as stories, but they are no better than that. All nations that dream of it also think that, of all peoples in the world, they will have the best of it for themselves.

Epitome of Unselfishness in Duty: The position of the mother is the highest in the world, as it is the one place in which to learn and exercise the greatest unselfishness, as a duty. The love of God is the only love that is higher than a mother’s love; all others are lower.

The mother thinks of her children first and then of herself. But, instead of that, if the parents are always thinking of themselves first, as is now the fashion, the result is that the relation between parents and children becomes the same as that between birds and their offspring which, as soon as they are fledged, do not recognize any parents. Blessed, indeed, are the children who look upon their parents as Divinity manifested on earth.

Doing our very best, whatever be our Duty is Ethics-in-work: The only way to rise is by doing the duty next to us, and thus gathering strength go on until we reach the highest state. Thus, in the story retold by Vivekananda in his Karma Yoga, the Vyadha and the woman did their duty with cheerfulness and wholeheartedness; and the result was that they became illuminated, clearly showing that the right performance of the duties of any station in life, without attachment to results, leads us to the highest realization of the perfection of the soul.

The secret of work: Let the end and the means be joined into one. Having fixed the goal, forget the goal and focus on the means; the means being right, the end is bound to be attained. When you are doing any work, do not think of anything beyond. Do it as worship, as the highest worship, and devote your whole life to it for the time being.

To the grumbler all duties are distasteful: Nothing will ever satisfy him, and his whole life is doomed to prove a failure. It is the worker who is attached to results that grumbles about the nature of the duty which has fallen to his lot. To the unattached worker all duties are equally good, and form efficient instruments with which selfishness and sensuality may be killed, and the freedom of the soul secured. We are all apt to think too highly of ourselves. Our duties are determined by our deserts to a much larger extent than we are willing to grant. Competition rouses envy, and it kills the kindliness of the heart.

From Slavery to Freedom: We are all slaves to our desires and necessities! Everything that we perceive around us is struggling towards freedom, from the lifeless particle of matter to the highest existence on earth, the human soul. The whole universe is in fact the result of this struggle for freedom. It is under the impulse of this tendency that man lives and works; the good loves; the bad hates and robs. When the line of action taken is not a proper one, not in harmony, Rtm, with nature’s plan, we call it unethical, evil; and when the manifestation of it is proper and high, we call it ethical, good. The freedom which the good seek is very different from that which the bad seeks. The freedom loved by the good leads him to sukh, shanti, Ananda; while that on which the bad has set his heart only enslaves us; forges other bonds of misery and want of that, for which he had been unethical!

Let us work on, doing as we go whatever happens to be our duty, and being ever ready to put our shoulders to the wheel. Then surely shall we see the Light!
