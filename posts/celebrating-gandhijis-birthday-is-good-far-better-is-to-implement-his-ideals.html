India to Bharat · Essay #188

Celebrating Gandhiji’s birthday is good; far better is to implement his ideals!

1. While we celebrate Mahatma Gandhiji’s birthday, we should ask ourselves, as enlightened citizens, as academia and, as those responsible for framing our educational processes: What actions have we taken in schooling and in governance, to implement his ideals. The reality is that schooling has failed to focus on this aspect of character and nation-building. Bharat’s culture is to harmonise both ‘material prosperity’ and ‘spiritual growth towards the highest’.

2. In his Hind Swaraj , Bapu asserts that our seers and sages laid the foundation of our national unity and Indian nationhood by establishing centres of pilgrimage on the four corners of India. In the process, they fired the imagination of our people with the idea of nationhood. He talks of the composite nature of Indian nationalism and rejects the British thesis that India was never a nation. People with different religious backgrounds would continue to constitute one nation so long as they maintain the principle of non-interference in one another’s religion.

2.1 But this secularism, according to Gandhiji, was to be based on ‘God’ with education focused to “bring our senses under our control and to help imbibe ethical behaviour in our life”; by ‘love-force’ or ‘soul-force’ securing rights by going through ‘personal sufferings’; observing ‘perfect chastity’ adopt ‘voluntary poverty’ ‘follow truth’ and ‘cultivate fearlessness’.

2.2 He avers that the ancient Indian civilization fits the bill for being the true civilization, which he defines as that ‘mode of conduct which points out to man the path of duty’. He adds that moral behaviour is nothing but attaining ‘mastery over one’s mind’. He admits that at present modern India is moving away from these old values!

3. Late PM Indira Gandhi took two major decisions to actualise the ideals of the Mahatma: Firstly, instead of focusing only on “Fundamental Rights” as was provided for in the Constitution, she took steps to add a most important Article 51A, on “Fundamental Duties”.

Secondly, just a few weeks before her demise, she took a path-breaking decision by declaring (GOI letter dated October 17, 1984), that the birthday of Swami Vivekananda is to be observed as National Youth Day every year from 1985 onwards as it was felt that the philosophy of Vivekananda, and his ideals for which he lived and worked, could be a great source of inspiration for the Indian youth’. The President of India and the then PM, Rajiv Gandhi, both addressed the youth on the first January 12, 1985 observance”.

4. The Fundamental Duties enjoin on us to become aware of ‘the rich heritage of our composite culture’; ‘cherish and follow the noble ideas which inspired our national struggle for freedom’; ‘develop scientific temper’; ‘strive for excellence’; ‘promote harmony & common brotherhood’; environment protection etc. Unfortunately, schooling has focused only on ‘marks’ and has failed to take steps to inculcate the spirit of Duties in the citizens.

4.1 It is only when our composite culture is understood and taught will we promote harmony and brotherhood. What is our composite culture? Who can tell us?

5. Gandhiji and Pt Nehru explain : I have gone through his (Vivekananda’s) works very thoroughly, and after having gone through them, the love that I had for my country became a thousand-fold – Mahatma Gandhi.

Directly or indirectly he has powerfully influenced the India of today. And I think that our younger generation will take advantage of this fountain of wisdom, of spirit and fire that flows through Swami Vivekananda – Jawaharlal Nehru.

The then Gujarat CM Narendra Modi (May, 2010), now PM, suggested that we should propagate Swami Vivekananda as a role model for the youth… the GOI should take initiatives for organizing befitting programs in about 150 universities all over world for spreading the messages of Swami Vivekananda’s teachings and ideas… His four folds of yoga ‘ Gyan, Bhakti, Raj and Karma ’ should be promoted in a big way’.

6. Essence of our composite culture : Each soul is potentially divine. The goal is to manifest this divinity within by controlling nature, external and internal (Mind). Do this either by work, or worship, or psychic control, or philosophy – by one, or more, or all of these – and Be free. This is the whole of religion. Doctrines, or dogmas, or rituals, or books, or temples, or forms, are but secondary details (Vivekananda).

He says: My whole ambition in life is to set in motion a machinery which will bring noble ideas to the door of everybody, and then let men and women settle their own fate. Let them know what our forefathers, as well as other nations, have thought on the most momentous questions of life. Let them see especially what others are doing now, and then decide. We are to put the chemicals together; the crystallization will be done by nature according to her laws’… Bring light to the ignorant, and more light to the educated, for the vanities of the education of our times are tremendous.

If a man with an ideal makes a thousand mistakes, the man without an ideal make fifty thousand; therefore, it is better to have an ideal. Let us know what our forefathers as well as other nations have thought on the most momentous questions of life; let them see what others are doing now and then decide. And this ideal we must hear about as much as we can, till it enters into our hearts, into our brains, into our very veins, until it tingles in every drop of our blood and permeates every pore in our body. We must meditate upon it.

7. Supreme Court (September 12, 2002) observed : Value-based education is likely to help the nation fight against all kinds of prevailing fanaticism, ill-will, violence, dishonesty, corruption, exploitation and drug abuse.

Although not the only source, religion is a major source of value generation. What is required is education about religion, their basics, values inherent in them, and comparative study of philosophy of all religions.

Education is necessarily a process of inculcating values to equip the learner to lead a life that is satisfying in accordance with cherished values and ideals of society – character development, integrated personality, life of great men, communities and saints exemplify values of self-discipline, survival in absence of material resources, simplicity, handling conflicts without violence, exploring simple but revolutionary ideas as mark of superior conduct and living.

National Commission of Secondary Education: The supreme end of educative process should be training of character and personality in a way that they will be able to realize their full potentialities and contribute to the well-being of the community. ‘If we exclude spiritual training we would be untrue to our whole historical development’. ‘We have to evolve a national faith, a national way of life based on the Indian outlook on religion, free from dogmas, rituals and assertions’. ‘schools can and must strive to resolve and sustain the universal and eternal values oriented towards unity and integration of people enabling them to realize the treasure within’.

8. The essential message of all religions is to inspire us to strive for the highest and the best by establishing us in the habit of daily swadhyaya (self-study) to know the highest and daily sadhna (self-development practices) to build powers and purity of mind to walk-the-talk to the highest. If we do not inspire the young to walk-the-talk during schooling then we cannot have good teachers and without good teachers, we cannot have good citizens!

9. The consequences of not focusing on ethics in education or governance! The educated and the elite in responsible positions are not committed to fulfilling their duties, to samaja, to organisation, to self, to family. They’re not inspired to strive for quality and continuous Improvements in work. They’re not inspired to lead a value-based life to feel ‘apanaapan, one’ with all. They’ve strong likes/dislikes; attachments/aversions. They’ve no self-control to desist from the habit of deliberately hurting or harming others. Without learning how to manage our responses and cope with negative thoughts and feelings people get into the habit of indulging in intoxicants, living unbalanced and undisciplined life; fault-finding, gossiping, becoming over-ambitious, jealous, self-righteous, egocentric. Schooling should teach us how to awaken our viveka, manage our thoughts and develop powers of the mind to walk towards the higher and better.
