India to Bharat · Essay #190

This is how our conquerors thought of us Hindus during the reign of Akbar!

1. This is how the official gazetteer, Abul-Fazl Allami, recording the state of affairs and happenings in Akbar’s Empire, in his famous ‘The A-in-i-Akbari’, has described us:

The people of this land are religious, affectionate, hospitable, genial and frank. They’re fond of scientific pursuits, inclined to the austerity of life, seekers after justice, contented, industrious, capable in affairs, loyal, truthful and constant. The godliness and self-discipline of these people is such as is rarely to be found in other lands.

The true worth of this people shines most in adversity and its soldiers know not retreat from the field. When the day is doubtful, they dismount from their horses and resolutely put their lives to hazard, considering dishonour of flight more terrible than death. They have no slaves. In times of distress, moreover, should anyone, though unconnected by ties of intimacy, implore their protection, they’re prompt to aid and grudge neither property, life nor reputation in his cause (recognition of atithidevo bhava in practice). They’re capable of mastering the difficulties of any subject in a short time and surpass their instructors, and to win the Divine favor they will spend body and soul and joyfully devote their lives thereunto.

2. They one and all believe in the unity of God, and as to the reverence they pay to images of stone and wood and the like, which simpletons regard as idolatry, it is not so. The writer of these pages (Abul-Fazl) has exhaustively discussed the subject with many enlightened and upright men, and it became evident that these images of some chosen souls nearest in approach to the throne of God are fashioned as aids to fix the mind and keep the thoughts from wandering, while the worship of God alone is required as indispensable. In all their ceremonial observances (aka rituals) and usage, they ever implore the favour of the world-illuminating sun and regard the pure essence of the Supreme Being as transcending the idea of power in operation. Incidentally, his father was also a saintly scholar.

3. In a Chapter, he calls ‘Comers into India’ Abul-Fazl extensively narrates the incident relating to the coming of Alexander, after defeating Porus in Punjab, to what he calls the country of Brahmans. He reports, quoting Firdausi, that the chiefs of that region told him that if the conqueror sought riches and worldly goods they were destitute of these; ‘Wisdom and knowledge dwell with us, nor cease to fill our bosom with untroubled peace… if thy design be gathering of knowledge and the search for truth, let those who seek it come not in this guise (as soldiers)’.

3.1 Alexander, therefore, leaving his army, set out with a few men and a discussion was held after which they wished Alexander that he may live forever, which of course he pointed out was inconsistent with mortality! Then the brahmans rejoined, ‘if the instability of worldly things is so evident to your Majesty, why this tyrannous oppression of mankind. Alexander for a moment bowed his head in humility and imputed his actions to the decree of fate.

3.2 Abul describes in detail, another version of this meeting, where the Brahmanas replied to Alexander, what can satisfy a man like you to whom the possession of the world is insufficient? We, on the other hand, enjoy no outward splendour, nor bodily vigour to fight you. Our goods are shared in common amongst us, our costliest robes are old and worn out. Our women are not in bondage to ornaments for the seduction of hearts and add nothing to the beauty inherited from their mother ( no make-up! ).

3.3 We’ve a king for dignity; not for the governance of justice or law. What would punishment serve in a land where none is wicked and there is no thought of crime? Alexander was struck by this and leaving them their freedom, abandoned his project. He wrote to the head of Brahmanas, Didim, ‘if what you say is true, I desire again to be informed of thy precepts and doctrines and in search of truth, may follow thy footsteps’. Didim replied, ‘what we’ve stated results from profound knowledge. You have not chosen to believe and you rejected it in our discussions. Now, therefore, with full knowledge believe my words. The brahman does not yield to promptings of desire. Content with his needs, he opens not the door of greed. Our food is what earth gives and intemperance has no place, for this reason, we’ve no need of medicine or physicians, and thus we enjoy perpetual well-being. We’re not indebted to each other. We’ve equality in all things; where’s the room for deprivation. In a land where the seeds of arrogance and vainglory grow not, universal poverty is consummate fortune. We’ve no governor, for our actions are not subjects for penal inquiry.

3.4 Our only religion is the worship of conscience, ethics (Viveka). We’ve the self-control to refrain from doing that which is against our conscience. We do not submit to the tyranny of the pursuit of wealth for it forces greed and brings misery in its train. We disdain idleness and hold it in reproach. We’re not averse from delights of wedlock by incapacity, for all things are in our power; so, we can forego them also. From sun we receive warmth, from dew moisture. Our thirst is met with streams and earth is our bed. We consider the body as subservient to Spirit within…. Of a truth, you’re the most unfortunate of mankind for your worship of land and wealth is sinful and your life is chastisement.

3.5 Alexander replied, if your words reflect the light of truth, I should infer that the brahmana alone are robed in the true characteristic humanity and are to be regarded as incorporeal spirits. But to hold that the natural tendencies of man are sinful is either to be God or to be jealous of the Supreme Being. I do not have the attributes of the Creator, and whosoever being blessed, abandons wrong actions and walks in the way of virtue, is not god, but by his grace rises above his fellowmen. True merit is to live simply amid fortune; I with all enjoyment at my command, have refrained from them altogether and have sternly chosen a life of toil, am more deserving of a glorious reward…. the profit that I have reaped from India has been my meeting with thee.

4. Abul-Fazl describes many things like the Hindu idea of the geography of the earth, the people, philosophy, mathematics, culture, caste system, learning, animal life, deity worship, Raja-Niti, etc. A few interesting facts:

Ruler as Virtuous Gardener: The ruler should avoid the evil of desire and anger; the Prince should be God-fearing, just, compassionate, ever ambitious of expanding his kingdom; protector of his subjects from harassment of tax-collectors, thieves, and evil-doers… A ruler is likened to a gardener and like a gardener the ruler should put away thorns and weeds and keep his flower-beds in good order. Likewise, from time to time the ruler should prune away, redundant leaves and branches and trim those who shoot too much above the flock; isolate those who become too powerful but nurtures the weak plants!

To find the circumference, the Hindus multiply the diameter by 3,927 and divide the product by 1,250… the gist is the ratio of 7:22. This Hindu rule was unknown to Greeks or they would’ve vaunted it in their own praise. Glory be to Him who alone knows this relation.

5. Conclusion: Wise and good men work to secure the blessings of God and the good opinion of mankind at the same time, and they succeed in both by their honesty, self-control, and unselfishness sense of duty. Abu-Fazl success in life illustrates this truth. Besides being a scholar, he rose to status of commander but was killed by those jealous of his wisdom.

Can our academia, religious preachers and political leadership unite and come together to train teachers to be mentors; such teacher-mentors, besides teaching various subjects, will be able to strategies how they can help inspire the young to imbibe such virtues that may take them Godward.
