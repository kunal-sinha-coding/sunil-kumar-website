India to Bharat · Essay #221

Insights from vedanta for worldly success

1. What is Success? Is it greater ‘status or wealth’ or is it ‘better character’ that defines success? Is it ‘material wealth’ or is it ‘virtues and commitment to kartavya-palan’ that defines success? Is a corrupt political leader, administrator, public servant, businessman, executive more successful or is an honest cobbler, sweeper, worker, farmer, housewife, teacher who silently do their duties, more successful?

2. Worldly Success: We will not try to define the idea of worldly success because, each of us are actually striving only for worldly success, in various fields. The fact is that not only are there infinite avenues and ideas of success, but our ability also differs equally widely. So, it may be better to say that as of now, each one of us are as successful as it was possible for us, under the circumstances.

3. The present ‘Indian-idea-of-success’ is of external, visible, ‘objective’, material things, like the Western culture. Certainly, as a householder, we must strive for all material comforts and we need to absorb the best of science and technology of the West. But what after we have taken care of Roti-kapada-makan, our basic necessity?

4. What do I do henceforth? Only when this question arises within me, would I ask, what next and feel the need to reflect and explore the idea of ‘success’, if at all.

5. What is Vedanta led ‘Bharatiya-idea-of-success’? In brief, it focuses not so much on the ‘objective-results’ of ‘success’, but much more on the ‘subjective-means’, the ‘cause’ of ‘success’. It treats a butcher, sweeper, worker, who does his duties unselfishly, honestly and sincerely, more successful than a teacher, professional, ruler who is selfish, dishonest, and negligent in his duties. A Bharati is he, who is striving for the highest illumination, “Perfection”.

5.1 Thus, Bharat’s vision of a successful person is not judged by his external status, “worker or ruler” or “wealthy or poor” but by his conviction to the ideal: Thru Dharma (righteous conduct) ‘earn’ and thru dharma ‘enjoy’ so as to remain on the critical path to “Perfection”.

5.2 Considering the importance of ‘dharma’, righteous conduct, Education and training, was focused on establishing the young in self-development processes to ‘know’ and remain on the critical path to “Perfection”. The purpose of schooling was to inculcate commitment to power of goodness; to ‘dharma’, i.e. karatvya-palan, righteous conduct.

5.3 Peter’s Principle says that people tend to rise to their level of incompetence. However, based on Vedantic insights, Bharatiya society was structured to guide people in a ‘vocation’ in harmony with their inherent strengths and weaknesses and within their character and competence. This was because, elders of society had the Vedantic awareness that choosing a vocation where we could excel and serve society was the only path to “Perfection”, the ultimate goal of work and life.

6. Basic Insights from Vedanta which lead to the ‘subjective’ idea of success:

6.1 First things first: Vedanta presents the worldview of “Oneness” and focus on the “Subjective”. It states that in and thru’ all our striving we are ultimately seeking ‘fulfilment’. And, unless we grow in feelings of oneness with more and more till, we realize our oneness with the ‘whole’ creation, this state of poorna-ananda, “Perfection”, we will not be fully satisfied.

6.2 Measure of Success: Only thru’ unselfishly striving for quality and excellence in work and working in a spirit of worship for good of samaja, is it possible to grow and attain “Perfection”. And, this “subjective” growth towards “oneness”, “Unselfishness”, “Perfection” is the Measure of Success, not status; not money.

6.3 Without clarity on the “Whole” a Jigsaw puzzle cannot be completed: Similarly, unless we have a clear picture of the ‘whole’ creation, of purpose of work and life, we remain short-sighted, in a state of ‘perpetual purposelessness’ and treat short-term pleasures and material comforts, as success.

6.4 Vedanta gives a clear idea of the ‘whole’. It then suggests that we learn to choose the long-term good, the ‘road less travelled by’. It backs its worldview of ‘oneness’ by a rational logic, which we the modern IQ-generation, can intellectually appreciate. The vision of the ‘whole’ as suggested by Vedanta is the very foundation of all goodness, virtues, ethics and mother of all Religions and is termed as ‘sanatana-dharma’, i.e. those eternal and universal values and righteous conduct, following which, we remain on the critical path to our highest ‘Perfection’.
